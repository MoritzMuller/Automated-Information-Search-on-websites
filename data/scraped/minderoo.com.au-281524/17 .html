AboutThe Global Fishing Index is an independent assessment of global progress toward the United Nation's Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) target 14.4: to effectively regulate harvesting, end overfishing and restore fish stocks to sustainable levels.
MethodologyThe Global Fishing Index assesses global and country-level progress towards fisheries sustainability: are countries effectively managing fisheries to end overfishing and restore fish stocks to sustainable levels of abundance?
Key FindingsOver the past 50 years, the world has witnessed a massive decline in the health of its fisheries. Quite simply, we are removing fish from the ocean at a far greater rate than they can naturally replenish.
JapanJapan has a developed fisheries governance system that, where applied, has the capacity to ensure sustainable fishing. However, Japan has made limited progress to restore fish stocks to sustainable levels, with half of its catch from unassessed stocks, with unknown levels of sustainability.
Viet NamThere are critical gaps in Viet Nams governance system, resulting in limited capacity to ensure sustainable fishing. Additionally, Viet Nam has made negligible progress towards restoring fish stocks to sustainable levels, with nearly all its catch coming from unassessed stocks, with unknown sustainability status.
Russian FederationWhile the Russian Federation has some elements of fisheries governance, there is limited evidence of progress to restore fish stocks to sustainable levels. Much of the Russian Federations catch remains unassessed, while nearly half of its stocks are overfished.
IndiaWhile India has most elements of a developed fisheries governance system, it has made limited progress to restore fish stocks to sustainable levels. Clear gaps remain in the governance system and most of Indias catch comes from unassessed stocks, whose status is unknown.
MalaysiaMalaysia has made negligible progress towards restoring fish stocks to sustainable levels, with nearly all its catch coming from unassessed stocks, with unknown sustainability status. Additionally, while Malaysia has some elements of fisheries governance, critical gaps remain, and fisheries are at risk of decline.
NorwayNorway has made some progress to restore fish stocks and has a developed system of governance that, where applied, has the capacity to ensure sustainable fishing. Additional work is needed to rebuild overfished stocks and expand management coverage across all fisheries.
